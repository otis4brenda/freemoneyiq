How to Calculate Your Monthly Mortgage Payment

Your monthly mortgage payment is determined by four main variables: your loan amount, interest rate, loan term, and property taxes. The core formula uses your principal and interest to calculate a fixed monthly payment that pays off the loan over the chosen term. According to Freddie Mac, the average 30-year fixed mortgage rate in early 2026 is approximately 6.8%, which is the default used in this calculator.

The math behind it is called amortization — each monthly payment covers that month's interest first, then the remainder chips away at your principal. In the early years of a 30-year mortgage, roughly 80% of your payment goes to interest. By year 25, that flips.

What Is a Good Monthly Mortgage Payment?

A widely used rule of thumb is that your housing costs should not exceed 28% of your gross monthly income. So if you earn $6,000/month before taxes, keeping your mortgage payment under $1,680 is considered financially healthy. Many lenders use this same benchmark when qualifying borrowers.

That said, what's "good" depends on your full financial picture. If you have no other debt, you may comfortably stretch to 33–35%. If you have significant student loans or car payments, staying closer to 25% gives you more breathing room.

What Factors Affect Your Mortgage Payment?

Five things have the biggest impact on your monthly payment: loan amount, interest rate, loan term, property taxes, and whether you need PMI. Of these, your interest rate has the most leverage — a 1% difference on a $300,000 loan changes your payment by roughly $180/month and over $60,000 in total interest over 30 years.

Your credit score is the primary driver of your interest rate. According to FICO data, borrowers with scores above 760 typically receive rates 0.5–1.5% lower than borrowers in the 620–639 range — a difference that can cost or save tens of thousands over a loan's life.

How Much House Can I Afford in 2026?

A general guideline is to keep your total home price at or below 3–5x your annual gross income. On a $100,000 salary, that's a $300,000–$500,000 home. However, with rates at 6.8%, affordability is tighter than it was in 2020–2021 when rates sat near 3%. Use this calculator to work backwards — plug in what you can afford monthly and adjust the home price accordingly.

Don't forget closing costs (typically 2–5% of the loan), moving expenses, and an emergency fund for home repairs. Most financial advisors recommend having 1–3% of your home's value set aside annually for maintenance.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is included in a monthly mortgage payment?

A monthly mortgage payment typically includes principal, interest, property taxes (if escrowed), and homeowner's insurance — together called PITI. If your down payment is less than 20%, you'll likely also pay PMI (private mortgage insurance), which adds $50–$200/month until you reach 20% equity.

How much does a $300,000 mortgage cost per month?

At 6.8% on a 30-year term, a $300,000 loan has a principal and interest payment of about $1,956/month. With property taxes and insurance added, total monthly costs typically run $2,300–$2,600 depending on your location.

Does a higher down payment lower my mortgage payment?

Yes — a larger down payment reduces your loan amount and thus your monthly payment. It can also eliminate PMI if you reach 20% down, saving an additional $50–$200/month.

What is the difference between principal and interest?

Principal is the loan balance you're paying down. Interest is the lender's fee for the loan. Early in a mortgage, most of your payment goes to interest. Over time, more shifts to principal — this is amortization.

How do I lower my monthly mortgage payment?

Make a larger down payment, improve your credit score to get a better rate, choose a longer loan term, or refinance when rates drop. You can also appeal your property tax assessment to reduce that portion of your payment.
