What Is Tax Withholding and How Do You Adjust It?

Getting a huge tax refund every year sounds great — but it actually means you've been giving the IRS an interest-free loan. Here's how to optimize your withholding.

📅 June 2026  ·  7 min read  ·  Taxes

Every paycheck, your employer withholds a portion of your earnings and sends it to the IRS on your behalf. At the end of the year, you file a tax return, and if too much was withheld, you get a refund. If too little was withheld, you owe the IRS. Understanding how this system works — and how to tune it — can put more money in your pocket every month.

How Tax Withholding Works

When you start a new job (or any time you want to adjust), you fill out a W-4 form (Employee's Withholding Certificate). This form tells your employer how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck.

Your employer uses the information on your W-4 — combined with your salary and pay frequency — to calculate your withholding using IRS tax tables. The money is sent to the IRS throughout the year as prepayment of your annual tax bill.

Note: Withholding only applies to federal and state income taxes. Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%) are always withheld at fixed rates regardless of your W-4.

The W-4: What Changed in 2020

The IRS redesigned the W-4 in 2020, eliminating the old "allowances" system (you may remember claiming 0, 1, or 2 allowances). The new W-4 has five steps:

  1. Personal information — name, address, filing status
  2. Multiple jobs or spouse works — adjusts for households with multiple income sources
  3. Claim dependents — if you have children or dependents who qualify for tax credits
  4. Other adjustments — for deductions, additional income not from jobs, or extra withholding
  5. Sign and date

For most single-income households with a straightforward tax situation, only Steps 1 and 5 are required. Steps 2–4 are optional but can significantly improve accuracy.

Why Your Refund Isn't Free Money

A tax refund feels like a windfall, but financially it's the opposite. A large refund means you overpaid the IRS throughout the year — and the IRS doesn't pay you interest on that money. You essentially gave the federal government an interest-free loan for 12 months.

In 2026, high-yield savings accounts pay 4–5% APY. If your refund is $3,000, that's $120–$150 in interest you could have earned if you'd kept that money in your own account throughout the year.

The ideal withholding results in a refund close to $0 — or a small, manageable amount owed. That said, some people prefer a larger refund as a forced savings mechanism, and that's a legitimate personal choice.

Common Situations That Require a W-4 Update

  • Getting married or divorced — your filing status and household income change
  • Having a child — you may now qualify for the Child Tax Credit ($2,000/child)
  • Starting a second job or side gig — additional income often isn't automatically withheld enough
  • Spouse starts or stops working — household income changes affect your bracket
  • Major deductions change — buying a home (mortgage interest deduction) or large charitable gifts
  • Large refund or tax bill last year — a signal that your withholding needs adjustment

How to Adjust Your Withholding

To increase withholding (reduce refund or avoid a big bill): On your W-4, enter an additional dollar amount in Step 4(c) "Extra withholding." Even $25–$50 extra per paycheck can make a meaningful difference.

To decrease withholding (get more in each paycheck): Make sure you've claimed all applicable dependents in Step 3, and check that your filing status is correct. If you have significant deductions beyond the standard deduction, enter them in Step 4(b).

The IRS Tax Withholding Estimator at irs.gov is the most accurate tool for calculating exactly what to put on your W-4. It walks through your full situation and gives a recommended withholding amount.

What About Self-Employed and Freelancers?

If you're self-employed or freelancing, no one withholds taxes for you — you're responsible for paying estimated taxes quarterly (April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15). The general rule: pay at least 90% of your current year's tax liability, or 100% of last year's tax bill, to avoid penalties.

Self-employed individuals also owe self-employment tax (15.3% on net earnings up to $168,600 in 2026), which covers both the employee and employer portions of Social Security and Medicare.

The Bottom Line

Tax withholding is the system that ensures you pay your income taxes gradually throughout the year rather than all at once in April. Getting it right means neither a surprise bill nor an oversized refund — just smooth, accurate prepayment of what you actually owe. Review your W-4 any time your life situation changes, and aim for a refund close to zero so your money works for you all year long.
