Why Most Freelancers Undercharge

The most common mistake new freelancers make is looking at a salaried job paying $60,000/year and charging the equivalent hourly rate — about $29/hr. The problem? That $60K salary comes with benefits, paid time off, employer tax contributions, and no business expenses. As a freelancer, you're on the hook for all of it.

Once you factor in self-employment tax (15.3%), federal income tax, health insurance, software tools, and the reality that only 50–60% of your working hours are actually billable, that $29/hr rate leaves you earning far less than the salaried employee you're trying to match.

💡 Rule of thumb: Your freelance hourly rate should be at least 2–3x the equivalent employee hourly wage to account for taxes, expenses, and unpaid time.

The Real Cost of Being Self-Employed

When an employer pays a salaried employee $75,000/year, the actual cost to that employer is closer to $95,000–$105,000 once you add payroll taxes, benefits, and overhead. As a freelancer, you absorb all those costs yourself — which is exactly why your rate needs to reflect the full picture, not just the take-home target.

Here's what most freelancers forget to include in their rate calculations:

• Self-employment tax: 15.3% of net self-employment income (both halves of Social Security + Medicare)
• Health insurance: $300–$700/month if not covered by a spouse's plan
• Retirement contributions: No employer match means you fund it all
• Unpaid time: Vacations, sick days, admin work, client calls, proposal writing
• Software & tools: Design tools, project management, invoicing, communication

How Many Billable Hours Are Realistic?

A standard work year has about 2,080 hours (40 hrs/week × 52 weeks). But for a freelancer, a realistic billable portion is 50–65% of that — roughly 1,000–1,350 hours per year. The rest goes to non-billable activities that are still essential to your business.

If you're just starting out, lean toward 1,000 billable hours as your planning baseline. As you build systems, raise your rates, and reduce non-billable overhead, you can refine this number.

When to Raise Your Rates

You should review your freelance rate at least once per year. Clear signals that it's time to raise rates: you're booking out more than 6 weeks in advance, clients rarely push back on your quotes, or your expenses have increased without a corresponding rate increase. Most successful freelancers raise rates 10–15% annually to stay ahead of inflation and increasing experience value.

Project Pricing vs. Hourly Rates

Your hourly rate is a floor, not a ceiling. Once you know your minimum rate, you can use it to price projects more profitably. Estimate the hours a project will take, multiply by your rate, then add a 20–30% buffer for scope creep. Many experienced freelancers charge 1.5–2x their hourly equivalent when pricing project work — and clients often prefer it because the cost is predictable.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate my freelance hourly rate?

Add your desired income, business expenses, and estimated taxes to get total revenue needed. Divide that by your realistic billable hours for the year. The result is your minimum hourly rate — consider adding a 20% buffer for slow months.

What percentage should freelancers set aside for taxes?

Most US freelancers should set aside 25–30% of gross income. This covers self-employment tax (15.3%) plus federal income tax. If you're in a high-tax state like California or New York, budget closer to 35%.

Why is my freelance rate higher than what employees earn?

As a freelancer you pay both employer and employee portions of Social Security and Medicare, fund your own benefits and retirement, and have no paid vacation. These add 30–50% to the true cost of your labor compared to a salaried employee.

How many billable hours can a freelancer realistically work per year?

Plan for 1,000–1,400 billable hours annually. A 40-hour week has 2,080 potential hours per year, but only 50–65% are typically billable once you subtract admin, marketing, proposals, and downtime between projects.

Should I charge by the hour or by the project?

Both are valid. Use your hourly rate as a baseline for project quotes. Project pricing can be more profitable — clients pay for outcomes, not time. Estimate hours, multiply by your rate, add a 20–30% buffer, and price accordingly.
